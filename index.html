What is PCA trying to do?

PCA searches for a direction in the data such that when all points are projected onto that direction, they are spread as far apart as possible.

This spread is called variance. Maximising variance means maximising the amount of information preserved after compression.

Try each preset

Horizontal: slightly spread, but still not optimal.
Vertical: points collapse together on the 1D strip — most information lost.
PC1: points are maximally spread on the strip. This is the direction PCA finds automatically.
PC2: perpendicular to PC1. Captures remaining spread. Independent of PC1.

The data cloud is an elongated ellipse. PC1 is the long axis of that ellipse, PC2 is the short axis. PCA finds these axes automatically — even in 12 dimensions.

What is a dot product?

u · v = |u| |v| cos θθ = angle between the vectors

Geometrically, the dot product is the length of the shadow u casts onto v, scaled by |v|. When the vectors point the same way, the shadow is longest. When perpendicular, the shadow vanishes — dot product is zero.

Why PCA needs it

In PCA, v is the candidate PC direction (unit vector) and x is a wine's measurements. The dot product vᵀx gives one number: where does this wine land along axis v?

PC score of wine x = vᵀxcomputed using the dot product for every wine

PCA finds the v that makes those scores most spread out — i.e. maximises their variance.

Try Wine PC1: v = [0.46, −0.43] is a simplified PC1 direction. u = [3, 2] are wine measurements. The dot product is that wine's "body and richness" score.

What is the covariance matrix?

C = (1/N) Σ (xᵢ−m)(xᵢ−m)ᵀA symmetric n×n matrix capturing how every pair of variables moves together

C[i,j] measures whether variables i and j tend to rise together (positive) or move opposite (negative). The diagonal holds each variable's own variance.

Why it matters

C encodes the entire shape and orientation of the data cloud. The cloud's elongation axis is the eigenvector of C with the largest eigenvalue — and that is PC1.

PCA boils down to: build C, find its eigenvectors, sort by eigenvalue. That is all.

Notice the strong negative correlation between alcohol and density — as fermentation converts sugar to alcohol, wine density drops. And between fixed acidity and pH — more acid always means lower pH (definitional).

Cv = λv  →  C = VΛVᵀEigendecomposition gives us the PC axes (V) and their importances (Λ)
Real World

Where PCA is Used

PCA is one of the most widely deployed techniques in data science. Click any card to see how it is applied in that field.

PCA was invented by Karl Pearson in 1901 and independently developed by Harold Hotelling in 1933. It remains one of the most widely used techniques in data science over a century later — a testament to how fundamental the idea of finding directions of maximum variance really is.

Key scikit-learn attributes

pca.explained_variance_ratio_
Array of % variance explained per PC. Sums to 1.0
pca.components_
Eigenvectors (loadings). Shape: (n_components, n_features)
pca.explained_variance_
Actual eigenvalues (variance in each PC direction)
pca.fit_transform(X)
Fit PCA and return X projected onto PC axes
pca.inverse_transform(X_pca)
Reconstruct approximate original data from PC scores

Always standardise first. Use StandardScaler before PCA. Without it, variables with larger numerical scales (e.g. total SO₂ in mg/L) dominate the covariance matrix purely due to scale, not because they are more informative.

Choosing n_components

Retain enough PCs to explain 70–90% of total variance
Look for the elbow in the scree plot
For visualisation use exactly 2 or 3 components
Use PCA(n_components=0.90) to auto-select
